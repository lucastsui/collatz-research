Individual Bound\n\nSince each $T_m$ has operator norm bounded by $O(\\sqrt{2})$ (it is close to a unitary — each row has at most 2 nonzero entries of modulus 1), the product of $p$ such matrices has norm at most $2^{p/2}$. But $F(t/q)$ is a single entry of this product, so:\n\n$$|F(t/q)| \\leq \\|T_{p-1} \\cdots T_0\\|_{\\text{op}} \\leq \\prod_m \\|T_m\\|_{\\text{op}}$$\n\nEach $T_m$ has $\\|T_m\\| \\leq 1 + 1 = 2$ (submultiplicative bound), giving $|F| \\leq 2^p$, which is far too crude. A better bound: $T_m$ acts on $\\mathbb{C}^{k+1}$, and in the worst case, $\\|T_m\\| \\leq \\sqrt{2}$ (since each column has at most 2 entries of modulus $\\leq 1$). This gives $|F| \\leq (\\sqrt{2})^p = 2^{p/2}$, still crude.\n\nThe trivial bound is $|F(t/q)| \\leq \\binom{p}{k}$ (triangle inequality), which is much better for $k \\ll p$.\n\n### 8b. Cancellation in the Transfer Product\n\nFor a **generic** $t/q$, we expect significant cancellation in the matrix product, yielding $|F(t/q)| \\ll \\binom{p}{k}^{1/2}$ or similar. The key question is: for how many $t/q$ can $|F|$ be large?\n\n## 9. Modified Large Sieve for Transfer Matrix Sums\n\n### 9a. The Bilinear Form Approach\n\nFollowing the strategy of the \"large sieve for matrices\" (cf. work by Kowalski on the large sieve and families of automorphic forms, or the large sieve for Frobenius in algebraic geometry), we can formulate a large sieve for our transfer matrix product.\n\nDefine:\n$$\\mathcal{F}(\\alpha) = \\prod_{m=0}^{p-1} T_m(\\alpha), \\quad \\alpha = t/q$$\n\nand $F(\\alpha) = \\mathcal{F}(\\alpha)_{0,k}$.\n\nThen:\n$$|F(\\alpha)|^2 = |\\mathbf{e}_0^T \\mathcal{F}(\\alpha) \\mathbf{e}_k|^2 \\leq \\|\\mathcal{F}(\\alpha) \\mathbf{e}_k\\|^2 = \\sum_{r=0}^{k} |\\mathcal{F}(\\alpha)_{r,k}|^2$$\n\nSo:\n$$\\sum_{\\alpha \\in \\mathcal{A}} |F(\\alpha)|^2 \\leq \\sum_{r=0}^{k} \\sum_{\\alpha} |\\mathcal{F}(\\alpha)_{r,k}|^2 = \\sum_{\\alpha} \\|\\mathcal{F}(\\alpha) \\mathbf{e}_k\\|^2$$\n\nNow, $\\mathcal{F}(\\alpha) \\mathbf{e}_k$ gives the last column of $\\mathcal{F}(\\alpha)$. To bound the sum, we need to understand the dependence of $\\mathcal{F}(\\alpha)$ on $\\alpha$.\n\n### 9b. Unfolding the Product\n\nWrite $\\mathcal{F}(\\alpha) = T_{p-1}(\\alpha) \\cdots T_0(\\alpha)$. Split this into two halves:\n\n$$\\mathcal{F}(\\alpha) = B(\\alpha) \\cdot A(\\alpha)$$\n\nwhere $A(\\alpha) = T_{M-1}(\\alpha) \\cdots T_0(\\alpha)$ (first $M$ factors) and $B(\\alpha) = T_{p-1}(\\alpha) \\cdots T_M(\\alpha)$ (last $p-M$ factors), for some midpoint $M$.\n\nThen:\n$$F(\\alpha) = \\mathbf{e}_0^T B(\\alpha) A(\\alpha) \\mathbf{e}_k = \\sum_{r=0}^{k} B(\\alpha)_{0,r} \\cdot A(\\alpha)_{r,k}$$\n\nThis is a sum of $(k+1)$ terms, so by Cauchy-Schwarz:\n\n$$|F(\\alpha)|^2 \\leq (k+1) \\sum_{r=0}^{k} |A(\\alpha)_{r,k}|^2 \\cdot |B(\\alpha)_{0,r}|^2$$\n\nThis doesn't directly help with summing over $\\alpha$, but it reveals that the **effective number of \"frequencies\"** is at most $k+1$.\n\n### 9c. Explicit Large Sieve Formulation\n\nLet me try a different approach. Expand $F(\\alpha)$ as a sum over all $\\binom{p}{k}$ subsets:\n\n$$F(\\alpha) = \\sum_{I} \\omega_I(\\alpha)$$\n\nwhere $\\omega_I(\\alpha) = e(\\alpha \\cdot S(I))$ and $S(I) = \\sum_{j=1}^{k} 3^{k-j} \\cdot 2^{i_j}$.\n\nThen:\n\n$$|F(\\alpha)|^2 = \\sum_{I, I'} e(\\alpha(S(I) - S(I')))$$\n\n$$\\sum_{\\alpha \\in \\mathcal{A}} |F(\\alpha)|^2 = \\sum_{I, I'} \\sum_{\\alpha \\in \\mathcal{A}} e(\\alpha(S(I) - S(I')))$$\n\nThe inner sum $\\sum_\\alpha e(\\alpha \\cdot n)$ over Farey fractions $\\alpha = a/q$ with $q \\leq Q$ is a Ramanujan-sum type quantity. Collision Analysis\n\n### 10a. When Does $S(I) = S(I')$?\n\nWe need:\n$$\\sum_{j=1}^{k} 3^{k-j} \\cdot 2^{i_j} = \\sum_{j=1}^{k} 3^{k-j} \\cdot 2^{i'_j}$$\n\nEquivalently:\n$$\\sum_{j=1}^{k} 3^{k-j} (2^{i_j} - 2^{i'_j}) = 0$$\n\nThis is a Diophantine equation in the exponents. Factor out: letting $c_j = 2^{i_j} - 2^{i'_j}$, we need:\n\n$$\\sum_{j=1}^{k} 3^{k-j} c_j = 0$$\n\nwhere each $c_j$ is a difference of two distinct powers of 2 (or zero if $i_j = i'_j$).\n\nFor $S(I) = S(I')$ with $I \\neq I'$, there must be at least one index where they differ. This is a system where each \"digit\" in base 3 is constrained to be a difference of powers of 2.\n\n**Claim**: For \"generic\" parameters (and in particular when $p$ is prime and $k$ is not too large), collisions are rare.\n\n**Heuristic**: The values $S(I)$ range over roughly $[0, 3^k \\cdot 2^p]$, and there are $\\binom{p}{k}$ subsets. If the values were random, the expected number of collisions would be $\\binom{p}{k}^2 / (3^k \\cdot 2^p)$. When $k \\sim p/\\log_2 3$ (the critical case where $2^p \\approx 3^k$), this ratio is $\\binom{p}{k}^2 / 2^{2p}$, which could be small or large depending on exact parameters.\n\n### 10b. The weights $3^0, \\ldots, 3^{k-1} \\bmod q$ cycle with period $\\text{ord}_q(3)$. If both orders are $\\geq p$ (which happens when $q > 2^p$, which is too large to be useful), then the map is injective.
